# General Star Wars: Unlimited deck-building and play guide

Last reviewed: 11 July 2026.

This guide stores reusable knowledge for future sets and decks. It deliberately separates official construction rules from recommendations that should change after testing.

## 1. Constructed deck rules

For Premier-style and Eternal constructed play:

- Use exactly one leader and one base.
- Use a minimum 50-card draw deck. This project standardizes on exactly 50 for consistency.
- Use at most three copies of any unit, event, or upgrade identity unless card text explicitly overrides the rule.
- Cosmetic printings share the same gameplay identity and copy limit.
- A sideboard may contain up to 10 non-leader, non-base cards where the event structure permits one. Site recommendations currently export an empty sideboard.
- Leader and base icons supply aspects. Each missing aspect icon adds 2 resources to a card's effective cost.
- Double-aspect cards require two matching icons. Aspect matching must therefore use a multiset.
- An off-aspect card is usually legal but penalized, not illegal. Recommended decks use zero penalties unless the guide documents a deliberate exception.

Always check current rotation and suspension information separately. Construction validity and current format legality are different concepts.

## 2. Why sequencing matters

Players alternate one action at a time. Deck quality therefore depends on more than resource efficiency:

- A ready unit threatens value before the opponent's next action.
- When Played abilities, Ambush, direct damage, exhaust, bounce, and immediate attacks are valuable because they act before the opponent can respond with another action.
- Claiming initiative ends your actions now in exchange for acting first next round.
- The leader deployment round is a predictable tactical pivot. The deck should know whether it wants to deploy and attack immediately, deploy defensively, or delay.
- Two arenas force an explicit policy: contest, race, answer, or dominate space.
- Drawing two cards and optionally resourcing one each regroup phase makes flexible cards valuable and situational cards less punishing, but poor resource decisions still create dead future rounds.

## 3. Archetype vocabulary

Use one primary archetype and optional secondary tags.

### Aggro

Wins by shortening the game. It prioritizes cheap units, immediate damage, attack buffs, Raid, Saboteur, Overwhelm, and a low curve. It trades into enemy units only when that protects more future damage than attacking the base.

### Tempo

Develops a clock while denying efficient opposing actions. Exhaust, bounce, combat tricks, cheap removal, and When Played effects produce temporary advantage. Tempo must finish before temporary answers become card disadvantage.

### Midrange

Plays efficient threats throughout the curve and changes roles by matchup: defend against aggro, pressure control, and seek favorable exchanges against other midrange decks.

### Control

Extends the game, answers threats, protects or heals the base, and wins with resilient late-game cards. Control still needs enough early interaction to avoid dying with expensive cards in hand.

### Attrition/value

Makes exchanges leave residual advantage through When Defeated effects, healing, recursion, extra cards, multiple bodies, or resource acceleration. It overlaps control and midrange but emphasizes cumulative advantage.

### Ramp

Spends early tempo to gain resources or temporary purchasing power, then deploys stabilizing threats ahead of schedule. Ramp cards without payoffs—and payoffs without functional early turns—both produce bad hands.

### Go-wide or trait synergy

Maintains several units, often sharing Rebel, Imperial, Vehicle, Force, or another trait, then converts width into buffs, extra attacks, or payoff effects. It is vulnerable to sweepers and multi-unit trades.

### Champion/upgrade

Protects one durable leader or unit and stacks upgrades or shields. It creates a fast recurring threat but risks losing multiple cards to hard removal, bounce, or capture.

### Defensive tempo

Uses Sentinel, high HP, shields, or combat manipulation to dictate attacks, then counterattacks after stabilization. It is more proactive than pure control.

## 4. Start with a deck contract

Before choosing cards, write:

> This deck wins by ___ before/after ___, using ___ as its decisive advantage.

Every non-flex card should do at least one of the following:

1. advance the win condition;
2. protect the plan;
3. answer what stops the plan;
4. improve consistency;
5. provide a necessary arena or curve role.

Rarity and monetary value are signals, never substitutes for synergy or role coverage.

## 5. Baseline composition

These are starting ranges, not rules:

- 28–38 units in most aggro and midrange decks.
- Roughly 70% units in a conventional unit-focused list.
- 12–16 reliable early plays.
- 8–14 space units unless the deck has another credible space policy.
- 6–14 interaction cards, including units with immediate interaction.
- 2–6 finishers or high-impact top-end cards.
- 5–15 events in proactive decks; control can use more if early stability remains sufficient.
- 0–5 upgrades normally; dedicated upgrade decks may use more.

FFG intentionally concentrates units at costs 2 and 3 because decks require early plays. See [Behind Unlimited: Designing for Draft](https://starwarsunlimited.com/articles/behind-unlimited-designing-for-draft).

## 6. Cost-curve templates

Count effective cost after aspect penalties. Treat ramp events as ramp turns, not ordinary bodies.

| Printed/effective cost | Aggro | Tempo or midrange | Control or ramp |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 0–1 | 3–7 | 1–5 | 0–4 |
| 2 | 11–15 | 9–13 | 7–11 |
| 3 | 11–15 | 10–14 | 8–12 |
| 4 | 7–10 | 7–10 | 6–9 |
| 5 | 3–6 | 4–7 | 4–8 |
| 6 | 0–4 | 2–5 | 3–6 |
| 7+ | 0–3 | 2–5 | 5–10 |

Tune the total to exactly 50. A ramp deck may carry more top end, but it must still operate when ramp is not drawn.

Opening consistency for a six-card hand in a 50-card deck:

| Reliable early plays | At least one initially | Approx. after a full mulligan |
|---:|---:|---:|
| 10 | 75.8% | 94.2% |
| 12 | 82.6% | 97.0% |
| 14 | 87.7% | 98.5% |

## 7. Copy counts

- Three copies: defining early plays, engines, and broadly useful interaction.
- Two copies: expensive, conditional, unique, or awkward in multiples.
- One copy: situational, searchable, collection-limited, or poor before the late game.

One copy of a powerful card is a luxury, not a consistent engine. Strategy text should say so.

## 8. Arena planning

Every deck guide must state one space policy:

- Contest with enough efficient space units.
- Race space with a faster ground clock.
- Answer space using flexible or cross-arena interaction.
- Dominate space and force the opponent to respond.

Do not add weak space units merely to meet a quota. Count credible interaction alongside units when evaluating arena access.

## 9. Mulligans

Keep a plan, not merely playable costs.

- Aggro: an early unit, another development play, and preferably reach or a buff.
- Tempo: an early threat plus cheap interaction.
- Midrange: a turn-one play, a turn-two bridge, and interaction or a high-value follow-up.
- Control: early interaction or a defensive body, with no hand clogged by top-end cards.
- Ramp: ramp plus a meaningful payoff, while retaining a functional fallback.
- Upgrade: a durable host before multiple upgrades.

Guides should name actual priority cards and common send-back patterns.

## 10. Resource priorities

Classify cards as:

- Keep: central to the curve or matchup.
- Situational: keep only when its target or synergy is likely.
- Resource-first: redundant, narrow, or too expensive for the next several rounds.

Plan at least two rounds ahead. Preserve one plausible finisher in slower decks, and avoid resourcing all interaction against an archetype it is meant to answer.

## 11. Initiative and tactical sequencing

- Attack with a threatened ready unit before playing another card.
- Delay high-information interaction until the opponent commits a target when possible.
- Claim initiative when next round's first action is worth more than remaining marginal actions.
- Plan the leader deployment round one round early.
- Do not overextend a wide board into an expected sweeper.
- Against aggro, calculate the damage race, Sentinel, and Restore rather than maximizing abstract card advantage.
- Against control, preserve pressure and force answers without committing every threat.

## 12. Scoring special decks

Gameplay is an expert input grounded in archetype strength, curve, synergy, role coverage, and known limitations. Other score components are derived.

Prestige:

```text
45% expert gameplay rating
+ 30% quantity-weighted rarity
+ 25% collector value
```

Rarity weights: Common 15, Uncommon 35, Special 50, Rare 70, Legendary 100. Collector value uses selected-printing prices from the dated public card database and is log-normalized against the most valuable deck in the active portfolio.

Showcase:

```text
55% expert deck quality
+ 30% non-Standard main-deck density
+ 15% cosmetic variant variety
```

Cosmetics never override legality, curve, or essential roles.

## 13. Playtesting loop

1. Validate exactly 50 cards, copies, aspects, ownership, and printings.
2. Draw five opening hands and record mulligan failures.
3. Play against aggro, midrange, and control references.
4. Record dead cards, missed curve turns, uncontested arenas, and unused resources.
5. Change no more than three to six cards at once.
6. Revalidate and repeat.
7. Promote stable, cross-set conclusions into this guide; keep set-specific findings in the set guide.
